\section*{Abstract}
This dissertation investigates 
GPU-accelerated option pricing and Bayesian inference under 
stochastic volatility. In the first part, European options under 
the Black--Scholes model are priced using an explicit finite-difference 
solver and Monte Carlo simulation, implemented in Python and accelerated 
with Numba and CUDA. Numba improves CPU performance, while CUDA delivers 
the largest gains: the explicit GPU solver achieves speed-ups of up 
to $950\times$ over the CPU implementation, and the Monte Carlo solver 
reaches $182\times$ for the largest path count.

The second part extends the study to the Heston stochastic-volatility 
model. A Bayesian framework is developed to estimate model parameters 
and latent variance paths from synthetic and real market data, and 
OpenEvolve is used to refine priors and inference code through 
execution-based scoring. The evolved models improve predictive fit, 
although exact parameter recovery remains difficult because of the 
identifiability and geometry of the Heston model.

Overall, the dissertation shows that GPU-acceleration can 
substantially improve option-pricing performance and that 
agent-assisted evolution can support Bayesian inference for 
stochastic-volatility models.

\chapter{Introduction and Problem Area}
Accurate and efficient derivative pricing is fundamental
in financial markets, as it underpins trading strategies, 
hedging, and risk management. The Black--Scholes Equation (BSE) 
remains one of the most influential models for 
pricing European options~\cite{black1973pricing}.

Although the Black--Scholes model admits a 
closed-form solution for standard European options, 
numerical methods remain important in computational 
finance. In particular, finite-difference methods provide 
a practical way to approximate solutions of the 
Black--Scholes equation has been widely studied 
in the literature~\cite{414b2dca-3879-3269-bb3d-fa223d68ead3,math8030391,5523590}. 
Certain options contracts admit no closed form solution
to the BSE due to the complex payoff function of the 
contract, which is why the equation needs numerical solutions.
Efficient pricing using numerical methods, 
requires many arithmetic operations, 
which can be time consuming. The explicit solution is
a good candidate for GPU-acceleration.  
Monte Carlo simulation offers a complementary 
approach that is especially useful for pricing 
exotic options, where the payoff structure 
is more complex~\cite{GOBET2009497,xu2014highperformancefinancialsimulation}. 
It is also well-suited to parallel computation, 
making it particularly attractive 
for GPU-acceleration~\cite{BOYLE197732,cpe2862}.

A key limitation of the classical Black--Scholes model 
is its assumption of constant volatility, which is often 
inconsistent with observed market behaviour. This motivates
the use of stochastic-volatility models, such as the 
two-factor Heston SDE~\cite{heston2015}.

This dissertation investigates two related problems.
First, it studies the numerical pricing of 
European options by solving the Black--Scholes 
equation using the explicit finite-difference 
method. These implementations 
are accelerated using Numba and CUDA and are benchmarked 
to evaluate performance. Monte Carlo simulations
are introduced as an alternative pricing methodology
for European options as well. It is also accelerated using
CUDA.

Second, the dissertation extends 
the analysis to the Heston stochastic-volatility model 
and explores the use of OpenEvolve for parameter estimation. 
The remainder of the
dissertation is organised as follows. Chapter~\ref{chap:bg} presents the 
mathematical background, including geometric Brownian motion, 
the Black--Scholes equation, finite-difference methods, 
Monte Carlo simulation and the Heston model. 
Chapter~\ref{ch:imp} describes the implementation of the Python, Numba,
and CUDA components. 
Chapter~\ref{sec:Benchmarks} reports benchmarking results and testing evidence. 
Chapter~\ref{ch:ExtHeston} presents the Heston-model extension, including
Bayesian estimation, agent-driven evolution, and experimental results. 
Chapter~\ref{ch:6} defines the system requirements and specifications, and Chapter
~\ref{ch:appendix} provides the appendices.

\section{The Geometric Brownian Motion}
Financial asset prices are inherently uncertain, and capturing their stochastic behaviour is central to finance, particularly in pricing derivatives. 
The Geometric Brownian Motion is a fundamental model for stock price dynamics~\cite{alma991001559369708046}. It forms the foundation of the Black--Scholes framework and is widely used for derivatives pricing. It is governed by the stochastic differential equation
\begin{equation}
    dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t
    \label{eq:GBM}
\end{equation}
where $S_t$ is the stock price at time $t$, $\mu$ is the drift, $\sigma$ is the volatility, and $W_t$ is a standard Brownian motion.
To simulate how asset prices evolve, the SDE in (\ref{eq:GBM}) must be discretised. We use the log-Euler method~\cite{alma991003696519708046}. Let $x_t = \ln(S_t)$ denote the log-price process. Applying It\^o's lemma~\cite{alma991008758143208046} gives
\begin{equation}
  dx_t = \left(\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)dt + \sigma\,dW_t.
\end{equation}
Using the log-Euler method, this SDE is discretised as
\begin{equation}
  x_{n+1} = x_n + \left(\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}\,Z_{n+1},
\end{equation}
where $Z_{n+1} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. At each time step, a new independent sample of $Z_{n+1}$ is drawn, so the stochastic shock is independent across increments. Exponentiating, and using $S_{n+1} = \exp(x_{n+1})$, gives
\begin{equation}
  S_{n+1} = S_n \exp\left(\left(\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}\,Z_{n+1}\right).
  \label{eq:discGBM}
\end{equation}

A key property of GBM is that, by It\^o's lemma, $\ln(S_t)$ is normally distributed, which means $S_t$ is lognormally distributed (shown in~\ref{fig:gbm}), 
which ensures that simulated prices remain strictly positive. This is an essential property for its applications in finance.
Equation~\ref{eq:discGBM} is then used to simulate sample paths of the stock price. Figure~\ref{fig:gbm} shows 75 simulated geometric Brownian motion paths over 365 days.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \input{tikz/GBM.tex}
    \caption{Simulated GBM paths over a 365-day period with $\sigma = 0.1$ and $\mu = 0.2$.}
    \label{fig:gbm}
\end{figure}

\section{The Black--Scholes Equation and Options}\label{sec:BSE}
An option is a financial contract whose value depends on an 
underlying asset, such as a stock or a bond. In this project, 
we consider European options, which can be exercised 
only at maturity $T$~\cite{alma991008708403508046}. 
The two cases studied in this paper are the call and the put.
A European call gives the holder the right, 
but not the obligation to buy the underlying asset 
at the strike price $K$ on an agreed upon maturity date $T$. 
The payoff at maturity is
\begin{equation}
  V(S,T) = \max(S-K,0)
\end{equation}
where $S$ denotes the stock price at maturity.
A European put gives the holder the right, but not the obligation, to sell the underlying asset at the strike price $K$ on the maturity date $T$. Its payoff at maturity is
\begin{equation}
  V(S,T) = \max(K-S,0)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Derivation of the Black--Scholes equation}
The Black--Scholes Equation (BSE) is a 
Partial Differential Equation (PDE) 
widely used in finance to determine the price of European options.
The BSE depends on the key parameters: 
the risk--free rate ($r$), volatility ($\sigma$), 
stock price ($S$) and time to maturity ($T$)

The BSE rests on certain assumptions; the underlying asset evolves with the GBM~\ref{eq:GBM} with constant $\sigma$ and a constant $r$.
Additionally, no arbitrage opportunities exist, which means it is impossible to make a 
profit without taking on risk, and there are no 
transaction costs or taxes associated with owning 
the underlying.

With these properties in mind, it is now possible to construct the Black--Scholes equation (BSE).
Let $V = V\left(S,t\right)$ denote the value of an option at time $t$ and stock price $S$.
First, we can apply Itô's Lemma~\cite{alma991008758143208046} to~\ref{eq:GBM} we obtain:
\begin{align}
  dV = \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} dt 
  + \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} dS  
  + \frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial S^2} \left(dS\right)^2
\end{align}
Substituting $dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW$ and using $(dS)^2 = 
\sigma^2 S^2\,dt$ under It\^{o}'s rules, this simplifies to:
\begin{align}
    dV =
  \left(
  \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}
  + \mu S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}
  + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}
  \right)dt
  +
  \sigma S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}dW.
  \label{eq:ItoApplicationBSE}
\end{align}

To eliminate the stochastic term $dW$ in~\eqref{eq:ItoApplicationBSE}, we construct a delta-hedged portfolio.
Delta hedging involves buying or selling 
the underlying asset in amounts determined 
by $\Delta = \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}$~\cite{alma991008837579408046}. 
Let $\Pi$ denote a delta-hedged portfolio consisting
 of one long option position and a 
 short position of $\Delta$ units in the underlying asset:
\begin{equation}
 \Pi = V\left(S,t\right) - \Delta S
\end{equation}
The change in portfolio $\Pi$ over time $dt$ is as follows: 
\begin{equation}
    d\Pi = dV - \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\,dS.
\end{equation}
Substituting the expressions for $dV$ and $dS$:
\begin{align}
    d\Pi &= \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} 
              + \mu S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}  
              + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial S^2}
            \right)dt 
          + \sigma S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\,dW 
          - \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\left(\mu S\,dt 
          + \sigma S\,dW\right).
\end{align}

The stochastic terms $\sigma S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\,dW$ cancel, 
as do the $\mu S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}$ drift terms, leaving a 
purely deterministic expression:
\begin{align}
    d\Pi = \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} 
             + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial S^2}
           \right)dt. \label{eq:dPi}
\end{align}
Since $d\Pi$ contains no $dW$ term, the portfolio is instantaneously 
riskless. By the no-arbitrage principle, a riskless portfolio must earn 
exactly the risk-free rate $r$, otherwise an arbitrage opportunity would 
exist. This gives:
\begin{align}
    d\Pi = r\Pi\,dt = r\left(V - \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}S\right)dt.
    \label{eq:noarb}
\end{align}
Risk-neutral valuation is a key property of the BSE and explains why the parameter 
$\mu$ in the underlying GBM is absent from the BSE.
The delta-hedging argument presented renders 
Investors' risk preferences irrelevant.
In a risk-neutral setting, the expected return of all 
assets is the risk-free rate $r$~\cite{alma991008589012108046}.
Setting (\ref{eq:dPi}) equal to (\ref{eq:noarb}) and dividing through 
by $dt$:
\begin{align}
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} 
    + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial S^2}
    = r V - rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}.
\end{align}
Rearranging yields the \textbf{Black--Scholes equation}:
\begin{align}
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} 
    + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}
    + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial S^2}
    - rV = 0. \label{eq:BSE}
\end{align}

\subsection{Closed Form Solution}
A closed-form solution exists for European Call and Put options. The closed-form solution for a European Call Option is:
\begin{equation}
  C(S,t) = S\Phi(d_1) - Ke^{-r(T-t)}\Phi(d_2)
\end{equation}
and for a put option, it is:
\begin{equation}
    P(S,t) = Xe^{-r(T-t)}\Phi(-d_2) - S\Phi(-d_1)
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{equation}
    d_1 = \frac{\log(S/X) + (r + 0.5 \sigma^2)(T-t)}{\sigma \sqrt{T-t}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    d_2 = d_1 - \sigma \sqrt{T - t}
\end{equation}
and $\Phi(\cdot)$ denotes the cumulative distribution function of the standard normal distribution.
This formula will be used throughout as a benchmark when comparing
the accuracy of the explicit solution (\ref{sec:explicit}) and Monte Carlo~\ref{sec:monteCarlo}

\section{The greeks}
To quantify different aspects of risk in an option position, the term the Greeks is used. 
\subsection{Delta}
The delta ($\Delta$) of an options
contract is defined as the rate of change of the option 
price with respect to the price of the underlying 
asset~\cite{alma991008589012108046}. Delta can be defined as:
\begin{equation}
   \Delta =  \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}
\end{equation}
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \scalebox{0.72}{\input{tikz/Delta.tex}}
    \caption{Impact of Stock Price(S) on Delta ($\Delta$)}
    \label{fig:delta_greek}
\end{figure}
For a European call, the delta ranges from 0 to 1 with respect 
to the underlying price, while for a put, it ranges from -1 to 0. 
As seen in~\ref{fig:delta_greek}, delta changes most rapidly near the strike, 
where the option is at-the-money.

\subsection{Theta}
The theta ($\Theta$) of an option measures the sensitivity of the option to the passage of time, often referred to as \textit{time decay}. It is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \Theta = - \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}
     \label{eq:theta}
\end{equation}

The negative sign in~\ref{eq:theta} indicates that, 
all else equal, an option loses value as it approaches maturity. 
This arises from reduced uncertainty and, therefore, a 
diminishing value of optionality. As seen in~\ref{fig:theta_greeks}, 
time decay is not linear, and it accelerates as time to expiration approaches. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \scalebox{0.72}{\input{tikz/Theta.tex}}
    \caption{Impact of Stock Price(S) on Theta ($\Theta$)}
    \label{fig:theta_greeks}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Gamma}
The gamma $\Gamma$ of an option measures the rate of change 
in delta with respect to the underlying's price. 
It is the second-order derivative with respect to the asset price. 
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma =\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}
\end{equation}
Gamma is particularly important for delta hedging, as it 
quantifies how often a portfolio needs to be rebalanced to 
remain delta-neutral. A large Gamma indicates that
very frequent rebalancing is needed, while a low Gamma indicates the opposite. 
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \scalebox{0.72}{\input{tikz/Gamma.tex}}
    \caption{Impact of Stock Price(S) on Gamma($\Gamma$)}
    \label{fig:gamma_greek}
\end{figure}
Figure~\ref{fig:gamma_greek} shows that Gamma peaks around the Strike 
price at which the option transitions between in-the-money and out-of-the-money. As it moves further away from the strike, 
gamma approaches zero, indicating that delta is constant. 

The sensitivities, $\Theta$, $\Gamma$ and $\Delta$ appear 
directly in the BSE and the PDE can be written as:
\begin{equation}
    \Theta
+ \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \Gamma
+ rS\Delta
- rV = 0,
\end{equation}

\subsection{Implied Volatility}
Within the BSE~\ref{eq:BSE}, volatility $\sigma$ is the only 
parameter that is not directly observable in the market. 
Instead, it is inferred from the observed market price of 
an option and is therefore called the \textit{implied volatility}. 
More precisely, the implied volatility is the value of $\sigma$ that, 
when substituted into the Black--Scholes pricing formula, produces the theoretical option value equal to the market price of the option.

If $V_{\text{mkt}}$ denotes the observed market price, 
then the implied volatility $\sigma_{\text{imp}}$ is defined implicitly by
\begin{equation}
V_{\text{BS}}\left(S,K,r,T,t,\sigma_{\text{imp}}\right) = V_{\text{mkt}}.
\end{equation}
Since this equation cannot, in general, be rearranged
Algebraically to solve for $\sigma_{\text{imp}}$, numerical root-finding methods are used for the case of the paper; the BrentQ~\cite{scipy_brentq} function will be used. 
Implied volatility is widely used in practice because it 
provides a market-based measure of the uncertainty or expected 
variability of the underlying asset over the life of the option.

\section{Finite Difference Method for the BSE}
Finite difference methods (FDM) are used to approximate solutions to PDEs. 
This is done by replacing derivatives with finite difference approximations~\cite{alma991006173869708046} 
and in the case of the BSE, discretising the time and asset price 
domains into a grid~\cite{alma991008827533008046}.

The time domain is discretised into $M$ steps using
\begin{equation}
  \Delta t = \frac{T}{M}.
\end{equation}
The stock-price domain is discretised into $N$ steps using
\begin{equation}
  \Delta S = \frac{S_{\max}}{N}.
\end{equation}
We write
\begin{equation}
  S_s = s\Delta S,\quad s=0,\ldots,N,
  \qquad
  t_i = i\Delta t,\quad i=0,\ldots,M,
\end{equation}
and denote the grid approximation by
\begin{equation}
  V_s^i \approx V(S_s,t_i).
\end{equation}
The result is an $(N+1) \times (M+1)$ grid, where each grid point 
represents the value of the option at time $t_i$ and stock 
price $S_s$. The grid is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:explicit_stencil}.
\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\input{tikz/grid.tex}
\caption{Stencil for the explicit FDM. The unknown value $V_s^i$ is computed from the known values $V_{s-1}^{i+1}$, $V_s^{i+1}$, and $V_{s+1}^{i+1}$.}
\label{fig:explicit_stencil}
\end{figure}
Although the scheme is explicit, option pricing proceeds backwards 
in calendar time, the terminal payoff at $t_M=T$ is known, and the 
values at $t_i$ are computed from the values at $t_{i+1}$.

When approximating the solution to the PDE, appropriate boundary conditions
and terminal conditions need to be introduced. These prevent 
These conditions are derived from the payoff at maturity and the 
limiting behaviour of the option price for extreme values of 
the underlying asset.
For a European Call, the Boundary Conditions are:
\begin{align}
    C\left(0,t\right) &= 0 \forall t, \\
    C\left(S,t\right) &\approx S - Ke^{-r\left(T-t\right)} \text{ as } S\rightarrow \infty, \\
    C\left(S,T\right) &= \max\left\{S-K,0\right\}
    \label{boundaryConditionsCall}
\end{align}
For a European put option, the corresponding conditions are
\begin{align}
      P\left(0,t\right) &\approx Ke^{-r\left(T-t\right)} \forall t, \\
      P\left(S,t\right) &= 0 \text{ as } S\rightarrow \infty, \\
      P\left(S,T\right) &= \max\left\{K-S,0\right\}
    \label{boundaryConditionsPut}
\end{align}

\subsection{Explicit}\label{sec:explicit}

The explicit finite difference scheme is obtained by approximating 
the time derivative with a backward difference and the spatial 
derivatives with centred differences. Starting from the Black--Scholes equation,
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}
    + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^{2} S^{2} \frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial S^{2}}
    + r S \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}
    - r V = 0,
\end{equation}
we use the approximations
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} \approx \frac{V_s^i - V_s^{i+1}}{\Delta t},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} \approx \frac{V_{s+1}^{i+1} - V_{s-1}^{i+1}}{2\Delta S},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial S^{2}} \approx \frac{V_{s+1}^{i+1} - 2V_s^{i+1} + V_{s-1}^{i+1}}{\Delta S^2}.
\end{equation}
Substituting these into the BSE gives
\begin{equation}
  \frac{V_s^i - V_s^{i+1}}{\Delta t}
  + \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 S_s^2 \frac{V_{s+1}^{i+1} - 2V_s^{i+1} + V_{s-1}^{i+1}}{\Delta S^2}
  + rS_s \frac{V_{s+1}^{i+1} - V_{s-1}^{i+1}}{2\Delta S}
  - rV_s^{i+1} = 0.
\end{equation}
Rearranging yields the explicit update formula
\begin{equation}
  V_s^i = A_s V_{s-1}^{i+1} + B_s V_s^{i+1} + C_s V_{s+1}^{i+1},
  \label{eq:exp}
\end{equation}
where, using $S_s = s\Delta S$, \label{sec:coeffs}
\begin{equation}
A_s = \frac{1}{2} \Delta t \left(\sigma^2 s^2 - r s\right),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
B_s = 1 - \Delta t \left(\sigma^2 s^2 + r\right),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
C_s = \frac{1}{2} \Delta t \left(\sigma^2 s^2 + r s\right).
\end{equation}
The option price today is obtained from $V(S_0,0)$, usually by 
interpolating between the grid values $V_s^0$ around the current 
stock price $S_0$.

The explicit scheme can then be used to price an option. 
In the example below, the option parameters are $S_0 = 200$, $r = 0.0375$, $K = 210$, $\sigma = 0.15$, and $T = 0.5$. 
The resulting option value surface is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:explicit_surface}. 
One advantage of solving the PDE numerically is that it yields the option value over the entire stock price and time grid, 
rather than only at a single point.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{images/optionPlot_final.png}
    \caption{Option value surface produced by the explicit finite difference method.}
    \label{fig:explicit_surface}
\end{figure}

One limitation of the explicit FDM for solving the PDE is that it is only 
conditionally stable. Although the scheme is straightforward to 
implement, its stability and accuracy depend on satisfying the \textbf{Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy} condition of the form~\cite{tavella2000quantitative}
\begin{equation}
 \Delta t \leq \frac{(\Delta S)^{2}}{\sigma^2}.
\end{equation}

In addition, discretising the PDE introduces truncation error, 
which measures the discrepancy between the continuous PDE and 
its finite difference approximation. For the explicit scheme, the local truncation error is
\begin{equation}
  O\left(\Delta t + \Delta S^2\right),
\end{equation}
indicating first-order accuracy in time and second-order accuracy in space~\cite{alma991008540855508046}.

As shown in figure~\ref{fig:spatial_relative_error}, 
as the spatial step size $\Delta S$ decreases, the size 
of the grid increases. This increases accuracy as the price
by the explicit solver converges on the analytical price.

\section{Down and Out}
The explicit solution presented in~\ref{eq:exp} can be extended 
to barrier options. Here we consider a down-and-out European call 
option with barrier level $H$, where the option is immediately 
knocked out if the asset price falls to or below the barrier. 
To accurately price a Down-and-out, the key changes are in the boundary
conditions and payoff functions.


\begin{equation}
V\left(S,t\right) =
\begin{cases}
0, & S \leq H, \; 0 \leq t \leq T, \\
\max\left(S-K,0\right), & t = T, \; S > H, \\
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
V\left(S,T\right) = S - Ke^{-r\left(T-t\right)},  S \to \infty.
\end{equation}
A down-and-out call is typically less expensive than a 
vanilla European call because the barrier introduces an 
additional condition under which the option becomes 
worthless before maturity~\cite{alma991008837439108046}. 
This feature can be beneficial when a lower initial premium 
is desired. The plot shown below corresponds 
to an option with parameters $\sigma = 0.1730$, $r = 0.0375$, $

and barrier level $H = 270$

\section{Monte Carlo}\label{sec:monteCarlo}
Monte Carlo simulation provides an alternative 
approach to option pricing based on the discretised 
GBM in~\ref{eq:discGBM}. The central idea is to generate 
a large number of independent sample paths for the 
underlying asset, evaluate the option payoff at 
maturity for each path, and then average the 
discounted payoffs~\cite{wilmott1998derivatives}. 
For a European call option, if $S_T^{(i)}$ denotes 
the terminal asset price on the $i$th simulated path, 
then the Monte Carlo estimator based on $N$ paths is
\begin{equation}
    C(S,t) \approx e^{-r(T-t)} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \max\left(S_T^{(i)}-K,0\right).
\end{equation}

The accuracy of the Monte Carlo improves as the number of simulated paths 
increases, but its convergence rate is relatively 
slow, with standard error proportional to 
$N^{-1/2}$~\cite{alma991001559369708046}. 
Consequently, obtaining highly accurate prices
may require millions of simulations, meaning that 
improved accuracy comes at a significant computational cost.
To illustrate this cost, a European 
Call option is priced while varying the 
number of simulated paths. For this benchmark, 
the parameters are fixed at 
$S_0 = 274.80$, $K = 275.00$, $r = 0.0375$, and $T = 1$ 
year, with the remaining model parameters held constant.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \scalebox{0.85}{\input{tikz/cpuTimeTakenMC.tex}}
  \caption{Left:CPU runtime of MC as the number of paths increases. Right: Option Price returned by MC}
  \label{fig:MCcpuBenchmark}
\end{figure}

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:MCcpuBenchmark}, the CPU runtime increases rapidly as the number of simulated paths grows. Increasing the path count from $2^{16}$ to $2^{25}$ raises the runtime from $3.031$ ms to $2006.624$ ms. 
This demonstrates that, although Monte Carlo methods are simple and flexible, achieving higher accuracy on the CPU alone quickly becomes computationally expensive. Since each simulated path is independent, Monte Carlo methods are highly parallelisable and therefore well-suited to GPU-acceleration.

\section{Explicit Solver Python Implementation}
The implementation of the explicit solver followed the method outlined in Section~\ref{sec:explicit}. 
First, a two-dimensional grid was initialised to represent the option value as a function of stock price and time. 
The terminal and boundary conditions were then applied to the grid. Next, the coefficients of the 
explicit finite difference scheme were precomputed and stored in three arrays. 
Finally, the solution was obtained by stepping backwards through time and updating each interior point of the grid.

Because the spot price did not always coincide exactly with a grid point,
interpolation was used to obtain an accurate option value. 
In particular, \textit{CubicSpline}~\cite{2020SciPy-NMeth,scipy-cubicspline-docs} was used to estimate the option price at the required spot value.

A full implementation is available in the accompanying GitHub repository~\cite{yalcin_finalyearproject}.
The Python package uses the same code from~\cite{yalcin_finalyearproject} pip package is published to PyPi~\cite{yalcin2026bse}.

\subsection{Optimisation}\label{sec:PythonOpt}
The initial implementation serves as a baseline, as it relies only on arrays and standard NumPy operations. 
Before moving to GPU-acceleration, the Python implementation can be improved using the \textit{Numba} library. 
Numba provides a just-in-time (JIT) compiler via the \texttt{@jit} decorator, which translates Python functions into highly optimised machine code at runtime.

Numba was chosen because it provided the best balance 
between performance improvement, development speed, 
and minimal code changes for the explicit finite-difference
solver. Cython could potentially offer similar or greater 
CPU performance, but it would require more intrusive code 
refactoring and a more complex build workflow. 

Because NumPy array operations dominate the baseline implementation, 
it is a natural candidate for Numba acceleration.
Although NumPy is implemented in C, repeated calls from Python still incur interpreter overhead. 
Numba reduces this overhead by compiling the relevant numerical code directly, 
allowing the solver to achieve performance closer to that of low-level languages such as C.

Adding the \texttt{@jit} decorator yields a considerable speedup.
Results and benchmarks can be found in Chapter~\ref{sec:Benchmarks}

\section{Application of GPUs}
Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) were originally developed for graphics workloads, 
particularly three-dimensional rendering, but have since evolved into powerful programmable processors~\cite{Mantas2016}. 
They are especially well-suited to problems with high computational demands, 
substantial parallelism, and workloads in which throughput is more important than latency~\cite{Owens2008GraphicsPU}.

The performance difference between CPUs and GPUs arises primarily from their architectural design. 
CPUs are optimised for low-latency execution of a small number of complex tasks, 
whereas GPUs are optimised for high-throughput parallel execution of many tasks. 
As a result, GPUs are particularly effective for data-parallel problems, 
where many arithmetic operations can be performed simultaneously on independent data elements.

CUDA follows a \textit{single-instruction, multiple-thread} (SIMT) execution model, 
in which many threads execute the same instruction sequence on different data~\cite{5446251}. 
This is well-suited to option-pricing workloads such as Monte Carlo simulation, 
where each path can be evaluated independently. In practice, threads are organised into blocks,
which are scheduled onto \textit{streaming multiprocessors} (SMs), the main processing units of the GPU.
Each SM is responsible for executing groups of threads and managing resources such as registers and shared memory. 
Efficient GPU performance, therefore, depends not only on parallelism in the algorithm, 
but also on how effectively the workload is mapped onto the available SMs~\cite{4626815}.

CUDA also provides \textit{unified memory}, which offers a
single memory space accessible by both the CPU and GPU. 
This simplifies programming because data does not always 
need to be transferred manually between host and device memory. 
However, explicit memory management can still offer better control over performance, 
particularly in compute-intensive applications where transfer overheads must be minimised.

NVIDIA developed CUDA to enable general-purpose computing on NVIDIA GPUs using languages such as C and C++. 
In the CUDA programming model, code executed on the CPU is referred to as the \textit{host}, 
while code executed on the GPU is referred to as the \textit{device}. 
In this project, CUDA was used to implement the solver's main parallel components.

CUDA also provides a range of libraries that simplify GPU development. 
The main libraries used in this project were \textit{Thrust}~\cite{nvidia-thrust}, 
which was used for device-side vector operations and reductions, and \textit{cuRAND}~\cite{nvidia-curand-host-api}, 
which was used to generate random numbers efficiently on the GPU.


\subsection{Monte Carlo GPU Implementation}
Monte Carlo pricing is well-suited to GPU-acceleration because each simulation path can be evaluated independently. 
Two device vectors are allocated using the Thrust library: 
one stores the Gaussian random numbers used to generate terminal asset prices,
and the other stores the discounted payoff for each simulated path. 
Once the kernel has completed,
the payoffs are summed using \texttt{thrust::reduce}, and the result 
is divided by the total number of paths to obtain the Monte Carlo estimator.

Within the kernel, each thread first computes its global path index:
\begin{verbatim}
pathIdx = blockIdx.x * blockDim.x + threadIdx.x;
\end{verbatim}
This gives every thread a unique starting index. The total number of threads launched in the grid is then computed as
\begin{verbatim}
stride = blockDim.x * gridDim.x;
\end{verbatim}
A grid-stride loop is used so that the implementation remains flexible when the number of simulated paths exceeds the number of available threads:
\begin{verbatim}
for (path = pathIdx; path < numOfPaths; path += stride) {
    ...
}
\end{verbatim}
This means that each thread begins at its own unique path index, 
processes that path and then advances by the total number of threads in the grid. 
As a result, each thread may evaluate one or more simulation paths.

In the experiments, a block size of 256 threads was used. 
The number of blocks required is therefore computed as
\begin{equation}
    \text{grid size} =  \frac{\text{number of paths}}{256}
\end{equation}
This ensures that enough threads are launched to cover all 
simulation paths while still allowing the grid-stride 
loop to handle larger workloads efficiently.

\subsection{Why the Explicit Solver Can Also Benefit}\label{sec:ExplicitGPU}

Although Monte Carlo is the more obvious candidate for GPU-acceleration, 
the explicit finite difference solver can also benefit 
from CUDA because each time level contains a large
number of independent spatial updates. From Equation~\ref{eq:exp}, 
the value at stock-price grid point $s$ only depends on the 
three neighbouring values from the next time level:
\begin{equation}
V_s^i = A_s V_{s-1}^{i+1} + B_s V_s^{i+1} + C_s V_{s+1}^{i+1}.
\label{eg:stencil}
\end{equation}
This is a three-point stencil operation. 
For a fixed time level $i$, 
every interior stock-price point $s=1,\ldots,N-1$ can be computed 
independently once the next time row, $V^{i+1}$, is
known. The dependency between time levels means 
that the outer loop over 
time steps must remain sequential, but the inner 
loop over spatial grid points is data parallel.

This structure is reflected in the CUDA implementation. 
The function \texttt{priceBlackScholesExplicitCall} 
allocates two one-dimensional device vectors, 
\texttt{currentValues}
and \texttt{nextValues}, instead of storing the
full two-dimensional grid. The terminal 
payoff is first written by \texttt{terminalConditionKernel}.
The main time-stepping loop
then launches \texttt{fillInteriorKernel} once per time 
level and swaps the two buffers after each launch.

Inside \texttt{fillInteriorKernel}, each thread computes 
one spatial index using
\begin{verbatim}
index = blockIdx.x * blockDim.x + threadIdx.x;
\end{verbatim}
and then applies the stencil~\ref{eg:stencil}

The first CUDA version uses global memory for the stencil reads. This already provides parallelism, but it also generates redundant memory traffic. Adjacent threads require overlapping
data: thread $i$ reads \texttt{currentValues[i - 1]}, \texttt{currentValues[i]}, and \texttt{currentValues[i + 1]}, while thread $i+1$ reads two of those same values again. A
shared-memory version reduces 
this repeated global-memory access by 
staging a tile of \texttt{currentValues} 
in block-local memory. In CUDA, this can 
be written using dynamically
allocated shared memory:

\begin{verbatim}
extern __shared__ float tile[];
\end{verbatim}

Shared memory resides on chip (within the SM) and 
therefore has much lower latency than global memory~\cite{harris2013sharedmemory}. 
To exploit this, each block loads a contiguous section 
of the solution row into \texttt{tile}. 
Because the stencil update depends on neighbouring 
values, the block must also load one halo value on 
the left and one on the right~\cite{alma991008772845408046}. 
After a \texttt{\_\_syncthreads} barrier, each thread can 
read most of the values required for the stencil directly 
from shared memory rather than repeatedly accessing global 
memory. The numerical update is mathematically identical 
to that of the global-memory kernel; the optimisation 
changes only where the row values are read from.

The explicit solver is therefore less parallel than 
Monte Carlo across the full computation because time steps 
cannot be evaluated out of order. However, each time step exposes
substantial spatial parallelism.

\subsection{Benchmarking Methodology}

The CUDA implementations were benchmarked 
using CUDA events, which record timestamps on 
the GPU execution stream and report elapsed time 
in milliseconds. This is more appropriate than using 
a CPU wall-clock timer because CUDA kernel launches are 
asynchronous, so the CPU may continue execution before 
the GPU work has completed. By recording a start event 
before the measured section, a stop event afterwards, and 
synchronising on the stop event, the reported time 
corresponds to completed GPU work\cite{harris2012performance}.

For the Monte Carlo method, timing is performed in 
\texttt{runMonteCarloCall}. The timed section 
includes device-vector setup, 
cuRAND normal random number generation, 
and the CUDA kernel that computes discounted call payoffs.

For the explicit finite-difference Black--Scholes 
method a start event is recorded immediately before the 
backward time-stepping loop. After all time steps have 
been launched, the stop event is recorded and 
synchronised. This 
measures only the repeated 
finite-difference kernel loop. 
It excludes grid construction, memory allocation, 
terminal-condition initialisation, 
copying the final grid back to the host, 
and interpolation of the final option price.

CPU benchmarks were run on a
system with an Intel Core i7 CPU clocked at 2.30 GHz, while CUDA benchmarks used an NVIDIA A40 GPU. Speed--up is measured using the general relation

\begin{equation}
  \text{speedup} = \frac{t_{\text{baseline}}}{t_{\text{optimised}}}
\end{equation}
where $t_{\text{baseline}}$ denotes the runtime of the reference 
implementation and $t_{\text{optimised}}$ denotes the runtime of the 
accelerated implementation. For the Python benchmarks, the optimised implementation is the Numba version,
whereas for GPU benchmarks the optimised implementation is the shared-memory implementation.

For benchmarking purposes, the option priced is 
a European Call Option with stock price $S_0 = \$274.80$, 
strike price $K = \$275.00$, time to expiry $T = 0.0548$, 
volatility $\sigma = 0.1730$, and risk-free rate $r = 0.0375$.
For the explicit FDM benchmarking, the main variable which 
changed was $\Delta S = {3, 1.5, 0.75, 0.375}$.
As $\Delta S$ decreases the size of the grid increases.

\chapter{Extension to the Heston Model}\label{ch:ExtHeston}
The BSE described in~\ref{sec:BSE} assumes
that volatility $\sigma$ is constant, this assumption
is inaccurate with empirical market data. 

The Heston model is a widely used nonlinear
stochastic volatility model, but in practice, its variance process is unobserved (latent)~\cite{heston2015}. 
This makes inference a joint state- and parameter-estimation problem: given observed asset or option prices, we seek to recover both the 
model parameters and the hidden volatility trajectory. 
When the model is estimated from the asset price alone, inference 
is typically carried out under the \textit{physical measure}; when option prices are used, the model is calibrated to match 
market-implied expectations of future volatility~\cite{Cui2017}.
In this work, we focus on the first estimation paradigm. 
Reliable volatility estimates of the Heston model are required in 
applications such as option pricing, hedging, and risk management 
in quantitative finance~\cite{Als2012}.

Several papers have addressed this estimation problem. 
For example, in~\cite{WANG201714100} a recursive estimation method 
is proposed by combining a consistency-enhanced extended Kalman filter
with pseudo-maximum-likelihood estimation.
There is a noticeable trend to cast the Heston model as a state 
space model and use state estimation and filtering methods
(e.g., Kalman filter, particle filter)%
~\cite{BlancBlocquel2024,BaoFengZhang2025,Gruszka2022,Li2013}.
For example,~\cite{Gruszka2022} proposes an algorithm 
which alternates between filtering the latent volatility,
using fixed parameter values, and then updating the model parameters
by Bayesian regression conditional on that filtered volatility.

Bayesian estimation is particularly appealing for the Heston model 
because it allows prior information about the parameters and latent 
volatility to be incorporated directly into the inference problem. 
More importantly, it yields a posterior joint distribution over 
parameters and hidden states, rather than only point estimates (as in maximum 
likelihood estimation), 
thereby quantifying uncertainty and model credibility.
Moreover, Bayesian methods produce estimates of the posterior 
uncertainty on model parameters and hidden states, which is 
important in quantitative finance as it can affect option/derivative 
pricing, risk management, and hedging decisions 
~\cite{GuillaumeSchoutens2012}.

Few works perform a Bayesian estimation of the model parameters 
and latent variables simultaneously 
(see, for instance,~\cite{FruhwirthSchnatter2008}), 
as this problem comes with significant challenges in terms of 
identifiability, numerical stability, and computational cost. 
In particular, careful tuning of prior distributions, 
consideration of possible reparametrisations, and the imposition 
of structural constraints, such as the Feller condition, may be required. 
This process can be highly time-consuming.

% 5. Our approach and contributions
We approach Heston estimation as a Bayesian inference problem
for a nonlinear state-space system, with both the latent volatility
path and the model parameters inferred jointly from observed data.
Since this inference problem is sensitive to the choice of priors,
reparametrisations, and numerical constraints, we define a composite
score that combines fit to the observed data with posterior and MCMC
diagnostics, including convergence, effective sampling, and posterior
shape (gauging multimodality and ridge-like geometries);
see Section~\ref{sec:mcmc_evaluation}. 
Next, we use OpenEvolve~\cite{openevolve}, an open-source framework 
inspired by AlphaEvolve~\cite{AlphaEvolve2025} that improves code through
LLM-generated edits, evolutionary search, and execution-based scoring,
to evolve the choice of prior parameters and related hyperparameters. 
Overall, our approach addresses a practical open problem in
Heston estimation and provides a systematic tool for the modeller,
reducing the effort needed for manual tuning
while improving estimation quality.

\section{The Heston model}\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Stochastic differential equation model}
The Heston model~\cite{heston2015} is the following system of stochastic differential 
equations which describes the price of an asset, $S_t$, along with 
its volatility, $\sqrt{v_t}$
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
\dd S_t ={}& \mu S_t \dd t + \sqrt{v_t}S_t \dd W_t^S,
\label{eq:sde-1}
\\
\dd v_t  ={}& \kappa(\theta-v_t)\dd t + \xi \sqrt{v_t}\dd W_t^v,
\label{eq:sde-2}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
where the first equation is a geometric Brownian motion with stochastic variance, 
$v_t$. The second SDE is a CIR process, and $W^S$, $W^v$ are two different 
Brownian motions, possibly correlated, with $\dd W^S_t \dd W^v_t = \rho \dd t,$
for some correlation $\rho \in (-1, 1)$. 
A notable property of the variance dynamics in~\eqref{eq:sde-2} is mean reversion;
namely, $\E[v_t] = \theta + (v_0 - \theta)e^{-\kappa t}$, i.e., the expected variance 
goes to $\theta$ at an exponential rate $\kappa$ as $t\to\infty$. 
Next, we derive a discrete-time state-space approximation suitable for 
Bayesian estimation of the parameters.

\subsection{Discretisation}
We use the subscript $k\in\N$ to refer to the discrete versions of the above
continuous-time quantities.
Before we discretise, we apply It\^{o}'s lemma~\cite{oksendal2014stochastic} 
with $g(s) = \ln s$, for which $g'(s) = 1/s$ and $g''(s) = -1/s^2$; let us define 
$\Lambda_k = g(S_k)$. The application of the lemma gives 
\begin{equation}
\dd \Lambda_t = \left(\mu - \tfrac{1}{2} v_t\right)\dd t + \sqrt{v_t}\dd W_t^S.    
\end{equation}
In this way, upon discretisation, the original stock price,
$S_k$, will remain positive since $S_k = \exp \Lambda_k$. 
We can now discretise $\Lambda$ using the Euler method
to obtain
\begin{equation}
    \Lambda_{k+1} 
{}={} 
    \Lambda_k 
    + \left(\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}v_k\right)h 
    + \sqrt{v_k h} Z_k^S,
\label{eq:lambda-updates}
\end{equation}
with $Z_k^S \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$.
More advanced discretisation schemes are proposed in the seminal 
work of Andersen~\cite{Andersen2007}.

We can apply the Milstein discretisation to the variance equation. 
The vol-of-vol term is $\sigma(v) = \xi\sqrt{v}$, and 
$\sigma'(v) = \nicefrac{\xi}{2\sqrt{v}}$, 
so the Milstein discretisation is 
\begin{equation}
    v_{k+1} 
{}={} 
    v_k
    + \kappa(\theta - v_k)h 
    + \xi \sqrt{v_k h}Z_k^v 
    + \tfrac{1}{4}\xi^2 h((Z_k^v)^2 - 1),
    \label{eq:milstein-vol-updates}
\end{equation}
where $Z_k^v \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$
and $\operatorname{cov}(Z_k^S, Z_k^v) = \rho$.
To guarantee the nonnegativity of $v_k$ without introducing a nonsmooth 
function to the model (e.g, $[v]_+ = \max\{0, v\}$), we introduce the 
following smooth approximation of $[v]_+$:
\begin{equation}
    (v)_{a, +} = a^{-1} \left( [av]_+ + \ln\left(1 + e^{-a|v|}\right) \right),
\end{equation}
with parameter $a > 0$.
We then apply $({}\cdot{})_{a, +}$ to the  $v$-updates of 
~\eqref{eq:milstein-vol-updates}.



As seen in~\eqref{eq:lambda-updates} and~\eqref{eq:milstein-vol-updates},
the discretised Heston model is a nonlinear state space model with state 
$x_k = (\Lambda_k, v_k)$. 
In practice, only the log-price is observed, while $v_k$
is latent, so the output equation is $y_k = \Lambda_k + n_k$,
where $n_k$ is a measurement noise term, which accounts for market 
microstructure effects and imprecisely recorded prices~\cite{KanayaKristensen2015}; we assume that 
$n_k \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_n^2)$, 
for some known $r>0$.
The process noise is the vector $Z_k = (Z_k^S, Z_k^v)$. 
Note that we can introduce the change of variables
\begin{equation}
    Z_k = \left[\begin{smallmatrix}
    1 & 0 \\ \rho & \sqrt{1 - \rho^2}
\end{smallmatrix}\right]w_k,
\end{equation}
where $w_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$.
Hereafter, we shall treat $w_k$ as the process noise term.
The unknown constant parameters can be packed in a vector 
$\eta=(\mu, \kappa, \theta, \xi, \rho)$.
Overall, we have a discrete-time model of the form
\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        x_{k+1} {}={}& f(x_k, w_k; \eta),
        \label{eq:ss:x}
        \\
        y_k {}={}& [1 ~ 0]x_k + n_k.
        \label{eq:ss:y}
    \end{align}
\end{subequations}
Given observations $y_0, \ldots, y_{N-1}$
the objective is to estimate the parameter vector $\eta$, 
the full states $x_0, \ldots, x_{N}$, 
and the realisations of the process noise, $w_0, \ldots, w_{N-1}$.
To this end, we follow a Bayesian estimation approach,
which is introduced in the following section.

\section{Estimation}\label{sec:estimation}

In this section, we describe a Bayesian framework for the 
estimation of the parameters and hidden states of the 
Heston model from output data.
We then introduce score functions that describe the quality 
of the estimated model; these are then used to drive 
an evolutionary improvement of the estimated model.


\subsection{Bayesian parameter and state estimation}
Hereafter we use the notation $x_{0:N}=(x_0, \ldots, x_N)$.
By Bayes' theorem,
\begin{align}
    &p(x_{0:N}, w_{0:N-1}, \eta | y_{0:N-1})
    \notag\\
    &\quad{}\propto{}
    p(y_{0:N-1} | x_{0:N}, w_{0:N-1}, \eta)
    p(x_{0:N}, w_{0:N-1}, \eta).
\end{align}
Under the assumption that $x_0$ and $w_{0:N-1}$ are mutually independent,
$p(x_0, w_{0:N-1}) = p(x_0) \prod_{k=0}^{N-1} p(w_k)$ and taking 
into account~\eqref{eq:ss:x}, the joint prior can be written formally as
\begin{multline}
    p(x_{0:N}, w_{0:N-1}, \eta)
    \\[-0.2em]
    = p(x_0)p(\eta) 
    \prod_{k=0}^{N-1} 
    p(w_k)\delta(x_{k+1} {-} f(x_k, w_k; \eta)),
\end{multline}
where $\delta$ is the Dirac delta.
Next, by~\eqref{eq:ss:y},
\begin{align}
    &p(y_{0:N-1} | x_{0:N}, w_{0:N-1}, \eta)
    \notag
    \\
    &\hspace{3em}{}={}
    \prod_{k=0}^{N-1} p(y_k | x_k, \eta) 
    {}={} \prod_{k=0}^{N-1} p_n(y_k - [1 ~ 0]x_k)
    \notag
    \\
    &\hspace{3em}{}\propto{} \prod_{k=0}^{N-1} 
        \exp\left(-\frac{(y_k - \Lambda_k)^2}{2\sigma_n^2}\right).
\end{align}
Having determined $p(x_{0:N}, w_{0:N-1}, \eta | y_{0:N-1})$,
we can marginalise to determine $p(\eta | y_{0:N-1})$.
To this end, posterior inference is carried out using 
Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)~\cite{Brooks2011}.
MCMC generates samples from a Markov chain whose stationary distribution 
is the target posterior, $p(x_{0:N}, w_{0:N-1}, \eta | y_{0:N-1})$.
The designer selects priors for the parameters, $\eta$.


A satisfactory fit should yield a posterior that is numerically and 
statistically well behaved: we favour posterior marginals that are 
reasonably regular and, ideally, unimodal, since strong multimodality, 
ridges, and funnel-like geometries often indicate \textit{weak identifiability}
(cf. Fig.~\ref{fig:posterior-geometries}), 
which calls for either reconsidering the priors or reparametrisation 
of the model.

\subsection{Evaluation of MCMC estimation}\label{sec:mcmc_evaluation}

In this section, we shall introduce a series of 
\textit{score functions} that define the quality of an estimated 
posterior distribution.


\textit{Modality.} 
To gauge the unimodality or bimodality of a univariate empirical 
distribution, its bimodality coefficient (BC)~\cite{Pfister2013}
is sometimes used. BC is defined in terms of the skewness and kurtosis 
of the distribution and a value $\leq\nicefrac{5}{9}$ is taken 
to imply that the distribution is unimodal.
This is a heuristic and Pfister \textit{et al.} in~\cite{Pfister2013}
have pointed out the shortfalls of this criterion.
For multivariate distributions, it is more challenging to 
assess modality.


Here we propose a \textit{unimodality score} for two-dimensional 
empirical distributions. Suppose we have a distribution of 
vectors $z^{(1)}, \ldots, z^{(N)} \in \R^2$. Firstly, we approximate 
the empirical distribution by kernel density estimation with a 
Gaussian kernel, that is,
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{p}(z) 
    {}={} 
    \tfrac{1}{N}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N} 
        |H|^{-\nicefrac{1}{2}}
        k(H^{-\nicefrac{1}{2}} (z - z^{(i)})),
\end{equation}
where $k$ is the Gaussian kernel, and the symmetric positive 
definite matrix $H \in \R^{2\times 2}$
is determined using Scott's rule~\cite{Scott1992book}.
Next, we define the superlevel sets of $\widehat{p}(z)$ as 
\(
    \operatorname{lev}_{\geq \gamma} \widehat{p} 
    {}={}
    \{z\in \R^2 {}:{} \widehat{p}(z)  \geq \gamma\},
\)
for $\gamma \leq \widehat{p}_{\max} \coloneqq \max \widehat{p}$.

Next, we wish 
(i) to tell whether each $\operatorname{lev}_{\geq \gamma} \widehat{p}$ is \textit{connected} 
for $\gamma \in [\alpha \widehat{p}_{\max}, \widehat{p}_{\max}]$,
(ii) to write each superlevel set as a union of connected components,
\begin{equation}
    \operatorname{lev}_{\geq \gamma} \widehat{p} = \bigcup_{\iota = 1}^{n_{\gamma}} C_{\iota, \gamma}.
\end{equation}
Since $\operatorname{lev}_{\geq \gamma} \widehat{p}$ does not admit a closed-form representation,
we approximate each set $\operatorname{lev}_{\geq \gamma} \widehat{p}$ by a discrete set on a grid
and reduce the problem of assessing connectedness of that discrete approximation 
to the connectedness of the associated graph, $\mathcal{G}_\gamma = (V_\gamma, E_\gamma)$.
Each vertex in $V_\gamma$ corresponds to a grid point, and a pair of 
vertices are linked by an edge if the grid points are adjacent.
Using connected-component labelling~\cite{HeXiwei2017} we determine the number of components; 
each connected component corresponds to a distinct high-density region, 
and multiple components at high levels provide evidence against unimodality.
We set $\alpha = 0.7$ so that the analysis focuses on the high-density region 
near the peak and is less sensitive to low-density tails and low-likelihood areas.
Alongside, we compute the \textit{elongation} of each component $C_{\iota, \gamma}$, 
which we define to be the square root of the condition number of the empirical 
covariance matrix, that is,
\begin{equation}
    \operatorname{elong}(C_{\iota, \gamma}) = \sqrt{\kappa(\Sigma_{C_{\iota, \gamma}})},
\end{equation}
where $\kappa$ denotes the condition number, and 
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma_{C_{\iota, \gamma}}
    {}={}
    \tfrac{1}{|C_{\iota, \gamma}|}
    \sum_{z_i \in C_{\iota, \gamma}} 
        (z_i - \bar{z}_{C_{\iota, \gamma}})
        (z_i - \bar{z}_{C_{\iota, \gamma}})^\intercal,
\end{equation}
where $\bar{z}_{C_{\iota, \gamma}}$ is the centroid of $C_{\iota, \gamma}$.

\textit{Elongation penalty.}
Elongations up to about 2--3 can be considered unremarkable. 
Values in the range 3--10 would indicate substantial anisotropy,
but are not necessarily problematic for posterior distributions. 
Elongation larger than 10 may indicate a ridge-like or weakly 
identifiable posterior geometry and is therefore penalised maximally.
We, therefore, introduce the \textit{elongation penalty} of a 
component $C$ to be 
\begin{equation}
    \ell_e(C) = \min\left(1, \max\left(0, \frac{\operatorname{elong(C)}-3}{7}\right)\right).
\end{equation}
Note that elongations at large values of $\alpha$ (closer to the 
peak of the distribution) are more undesirable. Therefore,
to define an overall elongation penalty, we take 
an average weighted by $\gamma$. More specifically,
we take slices of the distribution at levels $(\gamma_j)_{j=1}^{M}$
and determine the \textit{overall elongation penalty} as 
\begin{equation}
    \ell_e = 
        \dfrac{         
            \sum_{j=1}^{M} \gamma_j \max_{\iota=1,\ldots, n_{\gamma_j}} \ell_e(C_{\iota, \gamma_j})}
        {
            \sum_{j=1}^{M} \gamma_j
        }.
\end{equation}

\textit{Multimodality penalty.}
Further, we define the multimodality penalty as 
the weighted average of the number of components, 
that is,
\begin{equation}
    \ell_m = \frac{
        \sum_{j=1}^{M} \gamma_j (n_{\gamma_j} - 1)
    }{
        \sum_{j=1}^{M} \gamma_j 
    }.
\end{equation}


\textit{Overall shape score.}
We combine the elongation penalty, $\ell_e$, and the 
multimodality penalty, $\ell_m$, to define an 
overall shape score as the weighted geometric mean 
\begin{equation}
    s_s = (1-\ell_e)^{w_e}(1-\ell_m)^{w_m},
\end{equation}
with parameters $w_e, w_m \geq 0$ with $w_e + w_m = 1$.


\textit{Convergence of MCMC.} The potential scale reduction factor,
also known as $\widehat{R}$, is used to check if the MCMC has converged.
Roughly speaking, $\widehat{R}$ compares the variance within each chain 
with the variance between chains. Ideally, we want $\widehat{R} = 1$ 
which indicates that all chains are sampling from the same 
stable posterior distribution, while 
values of $\widehat{R} \leq 1.01$ are acceptable, and 
values $\geq 1.1$ imply the chains have not converged. 
Vehtari \textit{et al.} recommend the critical value of 
$\widehat{R}_c = 1.05$~\cite{Vehtari2021}.
Here we introduce the score
\begin{equation}
    s_{\widehat{R}} = \frac{1}{1 + 25 (\widehat{R}-1)^{1.2}},
\end{equation}
where the effect of the exponent $1.2$ it to penalise 
less strongly values of $\widehat{R}$ very close to $1$.

\textit{Effective sampling.}
In MCMC, the posterior distributions are estimated by samples
produced by a Markov chain; therefore, they are correlated.
The effective sample size (ESS) of an estimated parameter 
quantifies the number of equivalent \textit{independent} samples.
In particular, the \textit{bulk ESS} focuses on the 
centre of the distribution, while \textit{tail ESS} focuses on 
the tail~\cite{Vehtari2021}. 
Let $\mathrm{ESS}_{\rm min}$ denote the minimum of the bulk and 
tail ESSs across all posterior quantities.
We define the effective sampling score 
\begin{equation}
    s_{\rm ess} = \frac{\mathrm{ESS}_{\rm min}}{\mathrm{ESS}_{\rm min} + 1000}.
\end{equation} 



\textit{Divergences.} Numerical divergences of the Hamiltonian 
MC sampler are highly undesirable as they can hint towards a ``bad''
(e.g., funnel-like) geometry, or a large step size of the solver.
We penalise the number of divergences, $\mathrm{div}$, strongly
by introducing the score 
\begin{equation}
    s_{\rm div} = \frac{1}{1+\mathrm{div}}.
\end{equation}

\textit{Posterior predictive score.}
To assess posterior predictive performance, we consider the posterior predictive
distribution
\begin{multline}
p(y_k \mid y_{0:N-1}^{\mathrm{meas}})
=
\int
p(y_k \mid x_k,\eta)\\
p(x_{0:N},w_{0:N-1},\eta \mid y_{0:N-1}^{\mathrm{meas}})
\,\mathrm d x_{0:N}\,\mathrm d w_{0:N-1}\,\mathrm d\eta.
\end{multline}
For each time index $t$, we draw posterior predictive samples
$y_k^{\mathrm{rep},(1)},\dots,y_k^{\mathrm{rep},(M)}$, 
and determine the corresponding 
$S_k^{\mathrm{rep},(j)} = \exp(y_k^{\mathrm{rep},(j)})$.
Let $l_k$ and $u_k$ denote the empirical $2.5\%$ and 
$97.5\%$ quantiles of $(S_k^{\mathrm{rep},(j)})_j$, respectively. 
We define the posterior predictive interval PPI$_{95\%, t} = [l_k, u_k]$.
We then define the interval penalty at time $t$
\begin{align}
\ell_{\rm ppi, 95\%}(t)
&{}={}
u_k - l_k
+20\;\mathrm{dist}(S_k^{\mathrm{meas}}; \mathrm{PPI}_{95\%, t}).
\end{align}
This encourages predictive intervals that are both narrow 
and well calibrated. 
We can now define the posterior predictive score as
\begin{equation}
s_{\mathrm{pp}}
=
\exp\left(
-
\frac{
\sum_{t=0}^{N-1}\ell_{\rm ppi, 95\%}(t)
}{
2\sum_{t=0}^{N-1}S_k^{\rm meas}
}
\right).
\end{equation}

\textit{Overall score.}
We define the total score as the average of the scores 
we introduced above with two ``deal-breakers'': values
$\widehat{R} > 1.1$ or $\mathrm{div} > 5$ lead to a zero 
score. We define
\begin{equation}
  s_{\rm tot}
  =
  1_{\mathrm{div}\leq 5}
  1_{\widehat{R} \leq 1.1}
  \frac{
  s_{\mathrm{pp}} + s_{\mathrm{div}} + s_{\widehat R} + s_{s} + s_{\mathrm{ess}}
  }{5}.
  \label{eq:total_score}
\end{equation}


\section{Agent-driven evolution}\label{sec:evolution}
In this section, we discuss how OpenEvolve~\cite{openevolve} is 
used to evolve the prior distribution of $\eta$ and $x_0$
to maximise the total score.
The evolution starts from an \textit{initial program}
which specifies the model parametrisation and 
prior distributions.
OpenEvolve keeps a database of candidate programs, 
uses LLMs to propose mutations to promising programs, 
evaluates the new code with a user-supplied evaluator, 
and feeds the results back into the database, so future 
prompts are conditioned on what has worked so far.
Following the AlphaEvolve architecture~\cite{AlphaEvolve2025},
the population is further split into multiple islands, 
meaning multiple semi-isolated subpopulations evolve 
in parallel rather than a single global population.
The MAP-Elites algorithm~\cite{MouretClune2015} is further 
used to balance quality and diversity in the population.

In this work, LLMs are used to make an educated guess of 
parametrisations and prior distributions that can lead 
to a good estimation, while an evaluator serves as an 
external critic by computing $s_{\rm tot}$.
The overall scheme makes this LLM-driven evolution 
resilient to LLM hallucinations and allows leveraging
state-of-the-art language models with a very large number of 
parameters and large context sizes
such as ChatGPT 5.x, Gemini, or Claude Opus.

\textit{Multistage evaluation.}\label{sec:multistageEval}
Since each MCMC run can be rather time-consuming, we want 
to be able to identify setups (parametrisations and 
choices of priors) that leads to a poor-quality estimation.
We, therefore, propose a multi-stage evaluator: 
in Stage 1, the evaluator simply checks whether the mutated 
program compiles, and whether any of the prior variances
are too low. This way, we can eliminate early overly 
informative priors without having to perform time-consuming 
MCMC operations.
In Stage 2, the evaluator runs MCMC with 10,000 draws 
after 10,000 samples for tuning and computes the total 
score in~\eqref{eq:total_score}.
A third stage with 20,000 samples for tuning and 20,000
draws can be used when called for by the problem geometry.

\textit{System prompt design.}\label{sec:promptDesign}
The evolution is driven by the total score,
$s_{\rm tot}$, while the LLMs are informed by the individual 
scores, which are computed by the evaluator, 
and a zero-shot code prompt that combines role prompting, 
system prompting, and contextual prompting~\cite{Boonstra2025} which specifies
(i) the objectives of the evolution,
(ii) constraints (what the LLM should not do),
(iii) suggestions and instructions.

\textit{Initial program.}\label{sec:priors}
Weakly informative priors we used in the initial program.
For $\mu$ we let $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\hat{\mu}, 0.5^2)$, where $\hat{\mu}$
is the arithmetic mean of $h^{-1}\log \Delta S_k$.
For the mean reversion rate, $\kappa$, we use a a positively-constrained
truncated Gaussian prior, that is $\kappa \sim \mathrm{TruncatedNormal}(2, 0.5; \kappa > 0)$. 
The long-running variance is assigned a log-normal prior, 
$\theta \sim \log\mathcal{N}(\log \hat{\theta}, 0.4^2)$,
with $\hat{\theta}$ being the empirical variance of $h^{-1}\log \Delta S_k$.
The vol-of-vol parameter is assigned an exponential prior 
$\xi \sim \mathrm{Exp}(2)$. 
We lastly choose
$v_0 \sim \log\mathcal{N}(\log \hat{\theta}, 0.5^2)$, 
implying a log-normal prior on $v_0$.

After 60 iterations, OpenEvolve made some significant changes to the code,
mainly to the priors. In particular, the estimated $\hat{\theta}$, was 
capped from below at $2 \cdot 10^{-4}$.
This change ensures that when the sample variance is very small, 
the prior centre does not collapse towards zero.
The prior of $\kappa$ was changed from a truncated normal
to a log-normal prior. 
This guarantees positivity by construction, avoiding the need for 
hard truncation and leading to a smoother parameter space for inference.
The prior on $\mu$ was widened by increasing its standard deviation to 
$0.7$ from $0.5$, that is, $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\hat{\mu}, 0.7^2)$.
The prior on the volatility-of-volatility parameter, $\xi$,
was changed from an exponential distribution to a log-normal one.
While both priors enforce positivity, the log-normal distribution 
avoids concentrating of mass near zero and promotes
moderate to large values of $\xi$.
The prior variance of $v_0$ was modified from 
log normal to a truncated normal prior centred around 
$\hat{\theta}$, with a standard deviation
bounded away from 0, that is
\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        v_0 \sim{}& \operatorname{TruncatedNormal}(\hat{\theta}, \sigma^2; v_0 \geq 0), 
        \\
        \sigma ={}& \max\left(0.8\,\hat{\theta},\, 0.03\right)
    \end{align}
\end{subequations}
OpenEvolve also modified the Heston discretisation by 
introducing smoothing into the $v$-updates by 
defining
\begin{equation}
v_{{\rm mid}, k} = \tfrac{1}{2}\left(v_k + v_{k+1}\right),
\end{equation}
and using $v_{{\rm mid}, k}$ rather than $v_k$ in the 
$\Lambda$-updates, that is,
\begin{equation}
    \Lambda_{k+1} 
{}={} 
    \Lambda_k 
    + \left(\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}v_{{\rm mid}, k}\right)h 
    + \sqrt{v_{{\rm mid}, k} h} Z_k^S,
\label{eq:lambda-updates:v2}
\end{equation}
According to a comment by the LLM, this is done 
to ``improve posterior predictive calibration.''

The scores shown in Table \ref{tab:stock:scores}
for the best program indicate a well calibrated model.
The simulated paths, sampled from the posterior 
marginal distribution of $S_t$ shown in Figure 
\ref{fig:ftse} show that behaviour of the observed time 
series is captured by the estimated Heston model.


\chapter{System Requirements and Specifications}\label{ch:6}
The requirements for this project are divided into two related systems.
The first is the Black--Scholes option-pricing framework, which includes
Python implementations of Monte Carlo simulation and the explicit
finite-difference method, together with CUDA implementations for
performance comparison. The second is the Bayesian Heston estimation
framework, which uses PyMC and OpenEvolve.

The purpose of this chapter is to define the expected behaviour of both
systems in a form that is feasible, testable, and linked to the
implementation. The full requirements tables are included in
Appendix~\ref{tab:black-scholes-system-requirements} and
Appendix~\ref{tab:heston-system-requirements}. This chapter summarises the
main specification decisions and explains how the requirements are verified.

\section{Scope}
The Black--Scholes must price European call and put options, compare numerical estimates
against analytical Black--Scholes prices, and measure the performance of
CPU, Numba, and CUDA implementations under controlled benchmark settings.
Barrier-option experiments are included as an extension of the finite
difference framework, but the main assessed implementation focuses on
European options.

The Heston component is intended to support research-style experimentation
with Bayesian parameter and latent-state estimation. It must load observed
price data, construct a Heston state-space model, run posterior inference,
evaluate the quality of candidate implementations, and persist outputs that
allow the results to be inspected after an OpenEvolve run.

\section{Functional \& Non Functional Requirements}
The system requirements can be divided into functional and non-functional requirements.
Functionally, the Black--Scholes framework must simulate geometric Brownian motion paths,
price European call and put options using both Monte Carlo simulation and 
an explicit finite-difference method, while applying the
correct terminal and boundary conditions,
compare numerical prices with the 
closed-form Black--Scholes solution, 
and generate benchmark data, plots, 
and CUDA-based implementations for 
performance analysis. 
The Heston estimation 
framework must load and validate price data, 
transform observations for Bayesian inference, 
estimate Heston parameters together with latent variance paths, 
run configurable MCMC sampling, compute diagnostic and posterior predictive measures, 
use OpenEvolve to improve candidate implementations.

The main non-functional requirements are accuracy, 
performance, reproducibility, robustness, and testability. 
Accuracy is essential because the project is used for numerical finance experiments 
and results must be validated against analytical benchmarks where possible.
Performance is important because a central aim of the project is to 
compare CPU, Numba, and GPU execution.


\section{Technical Constraints}
The Python components are implemented using the scientific Python ecosystem,
including NumPy, SciPy, Matplotlib, Numba, Pandas, yfinance, PyMC,
ArviZ, and Jupyter/IPython support. The CUDA components are implemented in
CUDA C++ and built using CMake, with the benchmark environment targeting an
NVIDIA GPU. The Heston estimator also depends on PyMC, PyTensor, NumPyro/JAX
where available, and OpenEvolve.

These constraints influenced the design of the system. Python was used for
rapid numerical development and plotting, Numba was used as a low-overhead
CPU optimisation route, and CUDA was used where the algorithms exposed
parallel work. For the Heston extension, PyMC was chosen because it provides
a flexible probabilistic-programming interface, while OpenEvolve was chosen
because candidate programs could be scored automatically by the evaluator.

\section{Acceptance Criteria and Verification}
Each high-priority requirement is verified through at least one of three
mechanisms: automated tests, benchmark experiments, or result
inspection. The Black--Scholes pricing requirements are verified by unit
tests for mathematical helpers and integration tests that compare Monte
Carlo and explicit finite-difference prices against analytical
Black--Scholes prices. The CUDA performance requirements are verified
through benchmark runs using a fixed hardware environment and reported
timings. The Heston requirements are verified by unit tests for data loading,
simulation, diagnostic scoring, and parameter sampling, together with
integration tests using saved inference data.

The detailed test evidence is listed in Appendix~\ref{apex:tests}. The
benchmarking evidence is presented in Chapter~\ref{sec:Benchmarks}, and the
Heston estimation results are presented in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\section{Traceability}
The requirements are traceable through the dissertation as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item requirements are defined in the tables in Appendix~\ref{tab:black-scholes-system-requirements} and Appendix~\ref{tab:heston-system-requirements};
    \item implementation decisions are described in Chapter~3 and in the Heston implementation discussion in Chapter~5;
    \item performance and numerical evaluation are reported in Chapter~\ref{sec:Benchmarks};
    \item Heston estimation quality is evaluated in Section~\ref{sec:results};
    \item automated test evidence is summarised in Section~\ref{apex:tests}.
\end{itemize}

This traceability is intended to show that the requirements are not only
stated, but also implemented, tested, and evaluated against the aims of the
project.

\chapter{Conclusion}
This dissertation investigated computational methods for option pricing and stochastic-volatility estimation. The first part implemented and
evaluated Black--Scholes option pricing using explicit finite differences and Monte Carlo simulation, with acceleration through Numba and CUDA.
The second part extended the work to Bayesian parameter and latent-state estimation for the Heston model, using OpenEvolve to improve model
implementations through execution-based scoring.

The experiments with the BSE showed that the explicit 
finite-difference method converges towards the analytical 
Black--Scholes price as the
spatial grid is refined, but at increasing computational 
cost. Numba substantially reduced the runtime of 
the Python implementation, while the
CUDA implementations demonstrated the benefit of 
GPU-acceleration for large numerical workloads. 
In particular, Monte Carlo simulation scaled
well on the GPU because individual paths are 
independent, whereas the explicit finite-difference 
solver exposed parallelism across spatial grid
points at each time level.

The BSE implementation could be extended to the 
multi asset BSE which prices options based multiple underlying assets~\cite{contreras2015solutionmultiassetblackscholesmodel}.
While the MC simulations 
could be extended to the exotic options with more
complex payoff structures~\cite{repec1392}. 

The Heston extension demonstrated the potential 
of evolutionary algorithms driven by LLMs to produce meaningful Bayesian estimates 
of the parameters and paths of the stochastic 
Heston model—a 
challenging task that otherwise requires significant effort by an expert.
The results are encouraging and can be extended to other stochastic models.
Firstly, the approach could be benchmarked against established state-space 
estimation methods for the Heston model, 
including extended Kalman filtering, 
particle filtering techniques~\cite{BlancBlocquel2024,BaoFengZhang2025,Gruszka2022,Li2013}.
The framework could be extended to the 
other models, such as the Bates model~\cite{Bates}, 
which extends the Heston model by adding a Poisson-driven jump component to asset prices.

\paragraph{Comparison with related methods and broader implications.}
The Heston-estimation framework developed in this project has not yet been benchmarked directly against established alternatives such as extended Kalman filtering, unscented Kalman filtering, particle filtering, or gradient-based calibration methods. Those methods are generally more mature and often less computationally expensive in production settings, whereas the present Bayesian approach offers greater modelling flexibility and explicit uncertainty quantification over both parameters and latent variance paths. The use of OpenEvolve adds a further research contribution by allowing priors and model structure to be refined automatically, but it also increases computational cost and complexity. For this reason, the current system is best viewed as a research-oriented framework rather than a direct replacement for standard industrial calibration pipelines.

From a broader perspective, the work has commercial and economic relevance because option-pricing and volatility models affect hedging, risk management, and computational cost in financial workflows. Faster GPU implementations may reduce runtime and infrastructure cost, but inaccurate calibration can introduce model risk and lead to poor downstream decisions if results are used uncritically. There are also legal and ethical considerations: market data must be used in accordance with licensing terms, confidential financial data should not be exposed to external AI services without appropriate safeguards, and AI-assisted modifications should remain subject to human verification. Accordingly, the system should be treated as a decision-support and research tool, not as an autonomous decision-making system.
